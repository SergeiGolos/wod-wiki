# Kettlebell Clean & Press / Snatch

**Category**: The Golos Method  
**Type**: Ballistic Strength / Power-Endurance  
**Difficulty**: Intermediate to Advanced

## Description

The kettlebell is the ballistic power pillar of the Golos Method. Two movements form the core of this work: the clean & press, and the snatch. Both are full-body, explosive lifts that develop hip power, overhead stability, grip endurance, and cardiovascular capacity in a way that isolation exercises cannot replicate.

The **clean & press** is a two-part movement: the kettlebell is explosively pulled from the ground to the rack position at the shoulder (the clean), then driven overhead to lockout (the press). It builds raw strength, especially in the posterior chain, upper back, and shoulders, while the rack position challenges core stability and breathing under load.

The **snatch** is the clean & press compressed into a single, continuous motion. The kettlebell travels from the ground to overhead in one explosive pull, with no pause at the shoulder. It is one of the most demanding single exercises in existence — a fusion of deadlift power, Olympic lift speed, and sustained cardiovascular output.

In the Golos Method, these movements are performed for reps, for time, or in interval structures. The goal is not a one-rep max — it is the ability to sustain power output across extended sets while maintaining technical precision.

## How to Train It

### Equipment Selection

- **Kettlebell weight**: Men typically start with 16 kg for cleans and 12-16 kg for snatches. Women typically start with 8-12 kg for cleans and 8-10 kg for snatches. These are general guidelines — let your technique and endurance dictate your starting point.
- **Common men's progression**: 16 kg → 20 kg → 24 kg → 28 kg → 32 kg
- **Common women's progression**: 8 kg → 12 kg → 16 kg → 20 kg → 24 kg
- **Surface**: A firm, flat surface with enough clearance to drop the bell safely if grip fails.

### Execution Cues

**The Clean**
1. Start with the kettlebell on the floor between your feet, handle aligned with your ankles.
2. Hinge at the hips, grip the handle, and set your back flat.
3. Explosively extend the hips and knees, pulling the bell upward in a vertical path close to the body.
4. As the bell rises, punch your hand around the handle and catch it softly in the rack position at shoulder height.
5. The rack position: elbow tucked, bell resting on the forearm and biceps, wrist straight, core braced.

**The Press**
1. From the rack position, take a breath and brace your entire body.
2. Initiate the press by driving the elbow slightly forward, then pressing the bell vertically.
3. Lock out with the arm fully extended, bicep near the ear, shoulder packed down.
4. Lower with control back to the rack position.

**The Snatch**
1. Set up as for the clean, but grip slightly wider and stand slightly farther back from the bell.
2. Hinge and explosively extend the hips, pulling the bell upward in a straight line.
3. As the bell passes chest height, punch your hand aggressively around the handle and drive the bell to lockout in one motion.
4. The catch should be soft — the bell should not slam into your forearm. This requires precise timing: punch late, not early.
5. Lower by flipping the bell over the hand and guiding it down in a controlled arc back to the floor or into the next rep.

### Programming Recommendations

**Beginner (learning the movements)**
- Practice cleans and presses separately before combining them
- 4-5 sets of 3-5 reps per arm, 2-3 days per week
- Do not attempt snatches until cleans are mechanically sound

**Intermediate (building volume)**
- 10-minute EMOM of 3-5 clean & presses per arm
- Introduce snatches with short intervals (20-30 seconds per arm)
- Track pace and aim for consistent output across all rounds

**Advanced (power-endurance)**
- Extended snatch sets: 3-5 minutes per arm without setting the bell down
- Double kettlebell clean & press for raw strength emphasis
- Sport-style training: 10-minute sets with pace targets

### Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **The clean**: Banging the forearm (punching the hand too early), swinging the bell away from the body (wastes energy), squatting the clean instead of hinging (wrong movement pattern).
- **The press**: Leaning back excessively (indicates weak core or too heavy), flaring the elbow to the side (shoulder impingement risk), pressing around the head instead of straight up.
- **The snatch**: Catching the bell with a bent wrist (painful and weak), slamming the forearm on every rep (timing issue), snatching with a straight arm (treating it like a swing — dangerous).

## Safety Considerations

Kettlebell ballistics demand respect. The snatch in particular has a steep learning curve, and poor technique will bruise your forearms and stress your shoulders. Start with a weight you can control perfectly for 10+ reps. Never train snatches to failure — a missed catch overhead with a heavy bell can result in injury. If you feel your grip fading during a long set, set the bell down safely rather than risking a drop. Those with lower back issues should prioritize hip hinge mechanics and may want to avoid high-rep snatch work until their pattern is bulletproof.

## Collectible Metrics

- **Reps per set**: Track per arm and total
- **Weight used**: Bell weight for each movement
- **Pace (rpm)**: Reps per minute during timed sets
- **Total volume**: Reps × weight, aggregated per session and per week
- **Snatch test**: Max reps in 5 or 10 minutes at a given weight
- **Quality rating**: Self-assess catch softness, hip power, and lockout stability on a 1-10 scale
